1A.1

Change in Tandem

Topic 1.1
What It Means

Two quantities change in tandem when one changes as a result of the other changing. On a graph, we describe how the output (y) changes as the input (x) increases.

The AP exam asks you to translate between verbal descriptions, graphs, and tables — not just identify up/down, but describe the nature of the change.

The Four Basic Behaviors — Know All Language Forms
Increasing
As x increases, y increases.
Graph goes upward left to right.
As input increases, output increases.
Decreasing
As x increases, y decreases.
Graph goes downward left to right.
As input increases, output decreases.
Increasing then Decreasing
Graph rises, reaches a local maximum, then falls. Behavior changes at the turning point.
Decreasing then Increasing
Graph falls, reaches a local minimum, then rises. Behavior changes at the turning point.
⚠️ AP language trap: The exam describes change in tandem verbally. "As the car's speed increases, fuel efficiency decreases" — you must match this to a decreasing graph. Practice translating real-world sentences into graph behaviors.
Intervals of Increase and Decrease — How to State Them

State intervals using the input variable (x), not the output. Use interval notation or inequality notation.

Correct Format
f is increasing on the interval (a, b)  ← open interval notation
f is decreasing for a < x < b        ← inequality notation

Both are acceptable. Use the input values (x-coordinates) at the endpoints — not the y-values.
❌ Common error: Stating the interval using y-values. "f is increasing from 2 to 8" when 2 and 8 are output values is wrong. The interval must use the x-values: "f is increasing on (1, 4)."
Practice · Change in Tandem
Problem — AP Style
The graph of a function f is shown. The graph increases from x=0 to x=3, reaches a local maximum at x=3, decreases from x=3 to x=7, and increases from x=7 onward.

(a) On what intervals is f increasing?
(b) On what intervals is f decreasing?
(c) Where does f have a local minimum?
1
(a) Intervals where f is increasing
f increases on (0, 3) and (7, ∞).
These are the x-intervals where the graph moves upward.
→ Use the x-values at the boundaries of each increasing segment. Always open parentheses at the turning points.
2
(b) Intervals where f is decreasing
f decreases on (3, 7).
3
(c) Local minimum
f has a local minimum at x = 7 — where the decrease ends and increase begins (a valley in the graph).
→ Local min: f changes from decreasing to increasing. Local max: f changes from increasing to decreasing.
Problem
A function g is decreasing on (−∞, −2), has a local minimum at x = −2, is increasing on (−2, 5), has a local maximum at x = 5, and is decreasing on (5, ∞).

(a) On what intervals is g increasing?
(b) Where are the local extrema and what type are they?
(c) A student says "g is positive on (−2, 5)." Is this necessarily true? Explain.
(a) State the interval where g is increasing using correct notation
(b) State each local extremum, its x-value, and type
(c) Explain why increasing on an interval does NOT mean the function is positive there
(a) g is increasing on (−2, 5)

(b) Local minimum at x = −2 (decreasing changes to increasing). Local maximum at x = 5 (increasing changes to decreasing).

(c) Not necessarily true. Increasing means the output values are getting larger as x increases — but the output could still be negative. For example, g could go from −10 to −3 on (−2, 5) — increasing but still negative the entire time. Increasing/decreasing describes direction of change, not sign of values.
Problem — Real World Context
A car's distance from home (miles) is modeled by d(t) where t is hours. d increases from t=0 to t=2, stays constant from t=2 to t=3, then decreases from t=3 to t=5, reaching d=0 at t=5.

(a) Describe in words what is happening during each time interval.
(b) On what interval is d decreasing?
(c) What does d=0 at t=5 mean in context?
(a) [0,2]: car is driving away from home. [2,3]: car is stopped (parked). [3,5]: car is driving back toward home.

(b) d is decreasing on (3, 5)

(c) d(5) = 0 means the car has returned home — distance from home is zero at t=5 hours.
1A.2

Average Rate of Change

Topic 1.2–1.3 · Exam Miss Alert
The Formula

The average rate of change (AROC) of a function f over an interval [a, b] is the slope of the secant line connecting the two endpoints. It tells you how much the output changes per unit of input change on average.

Average Rate of Change Formula
AROC = f(b) − f(a)b − a = ΔyΔx = change in outputchange in input

This is the slope formula applied to two specific points on the function.
⚠️ What AROC is NOT: AROC is not the slope at a single point (that's rate of change at a point). AROC measures the overall slope across an entire interval. A function can be increasing on an interval with a negative AROC if it starts high and ends lower — wait, no. Actually AROC is positive when f(b) > f(a). But on a non-linear function, AROC changes as the interval changes.
AROC from a Table
Example — from a table
x1357
f(x)410814
AROC on [1, 5] = f(5) − f(1)5 − 1 = 8 − 44 = 1

AROC on [3, 7] = 14 − 107 − 3 = 44 = 1

AROC on [1, 7] = 14 − 47 − 1 = 106 = 5/3
AROC from a Graph — The Secant Line

On a graph, AROC is the slope of the line connecting two points on the curve. Draw the secant line, then use rise over run.

The slope of the secant line connecting (a, f(a)) and (b, f(b)) is the AROC of f on [a, b].
Units of AROC — Always Include Them

AROC has units of [output units] per [input unit]. The AP exam tests whether you can interpret AROC in context.

Context Examples
Distance (miles) vs time (hours): AROC has units of miles per hour (mph)
Temperature (°F) vs time (hours): AROC has units of degrees per hour
Cost ($) vs items sold: AROC has units of dollars per item
Practice · Average Rate of Change
Problem — Table Version (AP Style)
The table shows the cost C(n) in dollars of producing n items:
n (items)0102030
C(n) ($)50130190270
Find the AROC of C on [10, 30]. Interpret in context.
1
Apply the AROC formula
AROC = C(30) − C(10)30 − 10 = 270 − 13020 = 14020 = 7
2
State the units
Output = dollars, Input = items → units = dollars per item
3
Interpret in context
"Between 10 and 30 items produced, the cost increases at an average rate of $7 per item."
→ The full interpretation needs: the quantity ($), the rate (per item), and the interval (between 10 and 30 items).
Problem
f(x) = x² + 2x. Find the AROC on [1, 4]. Then interpret what the secant line slope represents.
Step 1 — Calculate f(1) and f(4)
Step 2 — Apply AROC = [f(4)−f(1)] / (4−1)
Step 3 — What does this number represent geometrically?
f(1) = 1+2 = 3  |  f(4) = 16+8 = 24
AROC = (24−3)/(4−1) = 21/3 = 7

This is the slope of the secant line connecting the points (1, 3) and (4, 24) on the parabola. It means f increases by 7 units on average for each unit increase in x on [1,4].
Problem — AP MCQ Style
The height h(t) in feet of a ball after t seconds is given by: h(0)=6, h(1)=22, h(2)=30, h(3)=22, h(4)=6.

(a) Find the AROC of h on [0, 2].
(b) Find the AROC of h on [2, 4].
(c) What does the sign change between (a) and (b) tell you about the ball?
(a) AROC on [0,2] = (30−6)/2 = 12 ft/s (positive — ball rising)
(b) AROC on [2,4] = (6−30)/2 = −12 ft/s (negative — ball falling)

(c) The AROC changes from positive to negative, indicating that the ball reached its maximum height somewhere around t=2 and is now falling. The symmetry (same magnitude, opposite signs) confirms t=2 is the peak.
1A.3

Concavity & Rate of Change at a Point

Topic 1.3 · Exam Miss Alert 🔥
What Concavity Describes

While increasing/decreasing tells you the direction a function moves, concavity tells you how the rate of change itself is changing — is the function speeding up or slowing down in its increase or decrease?

Concave Up ∪
Rate of change is increasing.
Function is increasing at an increasing rate, OR decreasing at a decreasing rate.
Tangent lines lie below the curve.
Concave Down ∩
Rate of change is decreasing.
Function is increasing at a decreasing rate, OR decreasing at an increasing rate.
Tangent lines lie above the curve.
The Four Combinations — This Is What the AP Tests
BehaviorConcavityWhat It MeansReal Example
IncreasingConcave UpIncreasing faster and fasterAccelerating car
IncreasingConcave DownIncreasing but slowing downCar approaching a speed limit
DecreasingConcave UpDecreasing but slowing downBall slowing as it rolls uphill
DecreasingConcave DownDecreasing faster and fasterFree-falling object speeding up
💡 Memory shortcut for concavity: Concave up = cup holds water ∪ = rate of change increasing. Concave down = frown ∩ = rate of change decreasing. These shapes tell you if the function is "accelerating" or "decelerating" in its behavior.
Rate of Change at a Point — Approximating with AROC

The rate of change at a specific point (the instantaneous rate) can be approximated by computing the AROC over a very small interval around that point.

How to Approximate from a Table
To approximate the rate of change at x = 3 using nearby table values:

Use the closest available interval: AROC on [2, 4] ≈ f(4) − f(2)4 − 2

Or use just one side if the other isn't available: AROC on [3, 5] ≈ rate at x = 3

On the AP exam, this is often asked as "approximate the rate of change at x = 3 using the table." Use the symmetric interval around the point when possible.
⚠️ AP exam trap: The question may ask for rate of change "at x = 3" when the table doesn't have x = 3. Use the surrounding values — the symmetric interval gives the best approximation. If the table has x = 2 and x = 4, use those.
Concavity Changes the AROC — Understanding the Pattern

When a function is concave up, the AROC on successive equal-width intervals is increasing (each successive AROC is larger than the previous). When concave down, the AROC on successive intervals is decreasing. This is exactly how the AP tests concavity from a table.

Detecting Concavity from a Table — The AROC Method
If AROC on [0,2] = 3, AROC on [2,4] = 5, AROC on [4,6] = 8...
ARoC is increasing → function is concave up on that interval

If AROC on [0,2] = 8, AROC on [2,4] = 5, AROC on [4,6] = 3...
AROC is decreasing → function is concave down on that interval
Practice · Concavity & Rate at a Point
Problem — AP Style (Q10 type)
A function f has the following values:
x0246
f(x)391213
Is f concave up or concave down on [0, 6]? Justify using AROC values.
1
Compute AROC on each sub-interval
AROC on [0,2] = (9−3)/2 = 3
AROC on [2,4] = (12−9)/2 = 1.5
AROC on [4,6] = (13−12)/2 = 0.5
2
Determine if the AROC values are increasing or decreasing
3 → 1.5 → 0.5: the AROC values are decreasing.
3
State the conclusion
"Since the average rates of change on successive equal-width intervals are decreasing (3, 1.5, 0.5), f is concave down on [0, 6]."
→ Decreasing AROC = concave down. Note f is still increasing (all values rise), just slowing down. Increasing + concave down = increases at a decreasing rate.
Problem
t0123
P(t)100115140180
P(t) is a population (thousands). Is P concave up or concave down? Approximate the rate of change at t=1. Interpret both in context.
Step 1 — Calculate AROC on [0,1], [1,2], [2,3]
Step 2 — Are these ARoC values increasing or decreasing? What does that tell you about concavity?
Step 3 — Approximate rate at t=1 using symmetric interval [0,2]
AROC [0,1] = 15, AROC [1,2] = 25, AROC [2,3] = 40
15 → 25 → 40: ARoC is increasing → concave up

Rate at t=1 ≈ AROC on [0,2] = (140−100)/2 = 20 thousand people per year

Interpretation: The population is growing at an increasing rate (concave up — growth is accelerating). At t=1, the population is growing at approximately 20,000 people per year.
Problem — Verbal Description
A student says: "This function is increasing AND concave down on the interval [2, 8]."

(a) Describe in plain English what the function is doing on this interval.
(b) Sketch the general shape of such a graph (describe it in words).
(c) Give a real-world scenario that would have this behavior.
(a) The function's output is getting larger as x increases, but the rate at which it grows is slowing down. Each equal step in x produces a smaller increase in y than the previous step.

(b) The graph curves upward-left-to-right (rising) but bends like a hill on top (∩ shape), not like a bowl. It goes up, but levels off as it goes.

(c) A car accelerating then maintaining speed — distance increases but rate of increase (speed) is slowing. Or: a medication's effect after a dose — effect increases but with diminishing returns over time. Or: learning a new skill — improvement increases but the rate of improvement slows.
