Ellipses

Topic 4.6
Standard Form — Two Orientations
Ellipse Standard Form
(x−h)²a² + (y−k)²b² = 1

Center: (h, k)
If a > b: major axis is horizontal. Vertices at (h±a, k). Co-vertices at (h, k±b).
If b > a: major axis is vertical. Vertices at (h, k±b). Co-vertices at (h±a, k).
If a = b: circle (special case of ellipse)

a = semi-major axis (larger value). b = semi-minor axis (smaller value). The larger denominator tells you the orientation.

Building an Ellipse from a Table of Points — The Q19 Pattern

Given a table of points on the ellipse, identify the center, major axis length, and minor axis length. Look for the maximum and minimum x and y values — they give the extreme points.

From Extreme Points to Standard Form
Table gives: leftmost point (−1, 3), rightmost (9, 3), topmost (4, 8), bottommost (4, −2)

Center: midpoint of left/right = ((−1+9)/2, 3) = (4, 3)
Semi-major axis a = 9−4 = 5 (horizontal, since left-right span is wider)
Semi-minor axis b = 8−3 = 5 (wait, if both equal 5, it's a circle!)

If: left (−2, 3), right (8, 3), top (3, 7), bottom (3, −1):
Center: (3, 3). a = 8−3=5 (horiz). b = 7−3=4 (vert).
Standard form: (x−3)²25 + (y−3)²16 = 1

Problem — Q18 Type (Ellipse Vertices)
An ellipse has equation (x+2)²36 + (y−4)²16 = 1.

(a) State the center, semi-major axis a, and semi-minor axis b.
(b) Find all four vertices and co-vertices.
(c) State the domain and range.
1
(a) Read from standard form
Center: (h,k) = (−2, 4)
a² = 36 → a = 6 (under x, so horizontal major axis)
b² = 16 → b = 4
a > b → horizontal ellipse
2
(b) Four extreme points
Vertices (major axis, horizontal): (−2±6, 4) = (4, 4) and (−8, 4)
Co-vertices (minor axis, vertical): (−2, 4±4) = (−2, 8) and (−2, 0)
3
(c) Domain and range
Domain: [−8, 4]   (leftmost to rightmost x)
Range: [0, 8]    (bottommost to topmost y)
Problem — Q19 Type (From Table)
An ellipse passes through: (1, 3), (7, 3), (4, 7), (4, −1).
Write the equation in standard form.
Step 1 — Center: midpoint of the leftmost and rightmost x-extremes. Also midpoint of top and bottom.
Step 2 — a = distance from center to rightmost vertex (horizontal)
Step 3 — Write in standard form: (x−h)²/a² + (y−k)²/b² = 1
Center: x=(1+7)/2=4, y=(7+(−1))/2=3 → (4,3)
a = 7−4 = 3 (horizontal, left-right span=6)
b = 7−3 = 4 (vertical, top-bottom span=8, so b is larger → vertical major axis)

Since b>a: major axis is vertical. a²=9 (under x), b²=16 (under y).
(x−4)²9 + (y−3)²16 = 1
Blind — Elliptical Bridge (Q21 Type)
A bridge arch is in the shape of the upper half of an ellipse. The bridge spans 80 feet horizontally and the maximum height at the center is 20 feet. A truck that is 14 feet tall and 10 feet wide needs to pass under the bridge. The truck's center is on the road (the x-axis).

(a) Write the equation of the ellipse with center at the origin.
(b) At x = 5 (5 feet from center, where the truck's edge is), what is the height of the arch?
(c) Can the truck pass through?
(a) Horizontal span 80 → a=40. Max height 20 → b=20. Center at origin.
x²/1600 + y²/400 = 1

(b) At x=5: 25/1600 + y²/400 = 1 → y²/400 = 1−25/1600 = 1575/1600 → y²=394.3 → y≈19.86 feet

(c) The truck is 14 feet tall and 10 feet wide. At x=5 (the truck edge), the arch height is ≈19.86 feet. Since 19.86 > 14, yes, the truck can pass.

Hyperbolas

Topic 4.6 · 🔥 Q20 Miss
Ellipse vs Hyperbola — The One Symbol That Changes Everything

An ellipse has a + between the two fractions. A hyperbola has a −. That single sign difference completely changes the shape.

Ellipse
Closed, oval shape
(x−h)²a² + (y−k)²b² = 1
Both terms positive → bounded shape
Center (h,k), two closed loops
No asymptotes
Domain: [h−a, h+a]
Range: [k−b, k+b]
Hyperbola
Two open branches
(x−h)²a² − (y−k)²b² = 1
Subtraction → unbounded shape
Two separate branches opening left-right
Has asymptotes: y−k = ±(b/a)(x−h)
Domain: x≤h−a or x≥h+a
The Two Orientations of a Hyperbola
Horizontal Hyperbola (x-term first, minus after)
(x−h)²a² − (y−k)²b² = 1

Opens LEFT and RIGHT
Vertices: (h±a, k)   ← on the x-axis direction
Asymptotes: y − k = ±ba(x − h)
Vertical Hyperbola (y-term first, minus after)
(y−k)²b² − (x−h)²a² = 1

Opens UP and DOWN
Vertices: (h, k±b)   ← on the y-axis direction
Asymptotes: y − k = ±ba(x − h)
💡 Which way does it open? The positive term tells you. If (x−h)² is positive → opens horizontally (left-right). If (y−k)² is positive → opens vertically (up-down). Think: the branches open toward the positive term.
Horizontal hyperbola centered at (h,k). Vertices at (h±a, k). Asymptotes (red dashed) pass through center with slope ±b/a. Branches open outward and approach but never touch the asymptotes.

❌ The Q20 error pattern: Students confuse which direction the hyperbola opens — they see a²=9 and b²=16 and guess the larger denominator tells direction. For hyperbolas, it's the POSITIVE term that determines direction. (x−h)²/a² positive → opens left-right. (y−k)²/b² positive → opens up-down. The size of a² vs b² tells you the asymptote slope, not the orientation.

Problem — Q20 Type (Hyperbola Graphs)
Identify all features of: (y+1)²25 − (x−3)²9 = 1

Then sketch a description of the graph.
1
Identify orientation — which term is positive?
(y+1)² is positive (first term) → vertical hyperbola → opens UP and DOWN
→ The positive squared term tells you the direction of opening.
2
Center, a, b
Center: (h,k) = (3, −1) (from x−3 and y+1)
b² = 25 → b = 5 (under the y-term, vertical)
a² = 9 → a = 3 (under the x-term, horizontal)
3
Vertices — on the y-direction (vertical opens up/down)
Vertices: (h, k±b) = (3, −1±5) → (3, 4) and (3, −6)
4
Asymptotes
y − k = ±(b/a)(x − h) = ±(5/3)(x−3) − 1
y + 1 = ±(5/3)(x−3)
y = (5/3)(x−3) − 1   and   y = −(5/3)(x−3) − 1
5
Graph description
Center at (3,−1). Upper branch opens upward from vertex (3,4). Lower branch opens downward from vertex (3,−6). Both branches approach the asymptotes but never touch them.
Problem
An equation is given: (x+4)²16 − (y−2)²9 = 1

(a) State center, orientation, and vertices.
(b) Write the asymptote equations.
(c) State domain and range.
(a) x-term is positive → horizontal. Center from (x+4) and (y−2). a²=16, b²=9.
(b) Asymptotes: y−k = ±(b/a)(x−h). Substitute h, k, a, b.
(c) Horizontal → domain has gaps, range is all reals
(a) x-term positive → horizontal. Center: (−4, 2). a=4, b=3.
Vertices: (−4±4, 2) → (0, 2) and (−8, 2)

(b) y−2 = ±(3/4)(x+4)
y = (3/4)x+5 and y = −(3/4)x−1

(c) Domain: x≤−8 or x≥0. Range: all reals.
Blind — Identify the Conic
Identify each equation as ellipse or hyperbola, state orientation, and find all vertices:

(a) 9x² − 4y² = 36    (b) 4x² + 9y² = 36    (c) (y−3)²/49 − (x+1)²/25 = 1
(a) 9x²−4y²=36 → divide by 36: x²/4−y²/9=1
Hyperbola, horizontal (x-term positive). a²=4→a=2, b²=9→b=3. Center (0,0). Vertices: (2,0) and (−2,0). Asymptotes: y=±(3/2)x.

(b) 4x²+9y²=36 → x²/9+y²/4=1
Ellipse, horizontal (a²=9>b²=4). Center (0,0). Vertices: (3,0) and (−3,0). Co-vertices: (0,2) and (0,−2).

(c) (y−3)²/49−(x+1)²/25=1
Hyperbola, vertical (y-term positive). Center (−1,3). b=7, a=5. Vertices: (−1,3±7) = (−1,10) and (−1,−4). Asymptotes: y−3=±(7/5)(x+1).
4C.3

Parametric Conics — Ellipses & Hyperbolas

Topic 4.7 · Q22–Q24
Parametric Forms for Each Conic
Parametric Ellipse
x(t) = a·cos(t) + h     y(t) = b·sin(t) + k
Traces the ellipse (x−h)²a² + (y−k)²b² = 1

Uses cos²t + sin²t = 1 to eliminate t.
Parametric Hyperbola — The Q23 Miss
Horizontal hyperbola:
x(t) = a·sec(t) + h    y(t) = b·tan(t) + k
OR: x(t) = a·cosh(t) + h, y(t) = b·sinh(t) + k (less common in AP Precalc)

Uses identity: sec²t − tan²t = 1 → (x−h)²/a² − (y−k)²/b² = 1

Vertical hyperbola:
x(t) = a·tan(t) + h    y(t) = b·sec(t) + k
Uses: sec²t − tan²t = 1 → (y−k)²/b² − (x−h)²/a² = 1
❌ The Q23 miss: Students use cos/sin for a hyperbola (which gives an ellipse). A hyperbola uses sec and tan because the identity is sec²t − tan²t = 1 (with subtraction), not cos²t + sin²t = 1 (with addition). The sign of the identity matches the sign in the conic equation.
Converting Parametric → Rectangular for Conics

The elimination technique depends on which trig identity applies:

Elimination Strategy
For ellipse (cos/sin): isolate cos and sin, then use cos²t + sin²t = 1

For hyperbola (sec/tan): isolate sec and tan, then use sec²t − tan²t = 1

Example: x(t) = 3sec(t), y(t) = 2tan(t)
sec(t) = x/3, tan(t) = y/2
sec²t − tan²t = 1: (x/3)² − (y/2)² = 1 → x²/9 − y²/4 = 1 (horizontal hyperbola)

Then convert x(t) = 5tan(t), y(t) = 3sec(t) to rectangular form and identify the conic.

Horizontal hyperbola (x-term positive) → use sec for x, tan for y
a² = 16 → a = 4. b² = 9 → b = 3. h=1, k=−2.
x(t) = 4sec(t) + 1,   y(t) = 3tan(t) − 2
→ Verify: sec²t−tan²t=1 gives ((x−1)/4)²−((y+2)/3)²=1 ✓

Convert x=5tan(t), y=3sec(t) to rectangular
tan(t) = x/5, sec(t) = y/3
Use sec²t − tan²t = 1: (y/3)² − (x/5)² = 1
y²/9 − x²/25 = 1
3
Identify the conic
y-term is positive (y²/9) → vertical hyperbola. Center (0,0), b=3 (vertical), a=5 (horizontal). Vertices: (0, ±3).
Problem — Q22 Type (Rewriting as Implicit)
x(t) = 4cos(t) − 1, y(t) = 2sin(t) + 3. Rewrite as an implicit rectangular equation and identify the shape.
Step 1 — Isolate cos(t) and sin(t)
Step 2 — Apply the identity cos²t + sin²t = 1
Step 3 — Identify: is a²=b²? If not, which is larger?
cos(t)=(x+1)/4, sin(t)=(y−3)/2
((x+1)/4)²+((y−3)/2)²=1
(x+1)²/16 + (y−3)²/4 = 1

a²=16>b²=4 → horizontal ellipse. Center (−1,3). Semi-major a=4 (horizontal). Semi-minor b=2 (vertical).
Blind — Q24 Type (Hyperbola from Graph)
A graph shows a hyperbola with vertices at (−3, 0) and (3, 0) and asymptotes y = ±(4/3)x.

(a) Write the rectangular equation.
(b) Write parametric equations for this hyperbola.
(c) Which parametric form would produce an ellipse with the same a and b? Write it.
(a) Vertices (±3,0) on x-axis → horizontal hyperbola, center (0,0), a=3. Asymptotes y=±(b/a)x=±(4/3)x → b/a=4/3 → b=4.
x²/9 − y²/16 = 1

(b) Horizontal hyperbola → sec for x, tan for y:
x(t) = 3sec(t), y(t) = 4tan(t)
Verify: (3sec(t)/3)² − (4tan(t)/4)² = sec²t − tan²t = 1 ✓

(c) Same a=3, b=4, use cos/sin (+ identity → ellipse):
x(t) = 3cos(t), y(t) = 4sin(t)
This traces x²/9 + y²/16 = 1 (ellipse, same a and b, different shape).
