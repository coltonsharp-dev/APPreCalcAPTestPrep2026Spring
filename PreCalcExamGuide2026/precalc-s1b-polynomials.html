1B.1

Extrema & Points of Inflection

Topic 1.4 · 🔥 Biggest Miss
Local Extrema — Definitions

A local (relative) maximum is a point where the function value is greater than all nearby points. The graph has a "peak" there — it's increasing before and decreasing after.

A local (relative) minimum is a point where the function value is less than all nearby points. The graph has a "valley" there — it's decreasing before and increasing after.

How to Identify from a Graph
Local MAX → function changes from increasing to decreasing (a peak ∧)
Local MIN → function changes from decreasing to increasing (a valley ∨)

State local extrema as a point (x, y) or just the x-value depending on what the question asks.
⚠️ Local ≠ Global (Absolute). A local max is the highest point in its neighborhood. The global (absolute) maximum is the highest point on the entire domain. A polynomial can have multiple local maxima but only one global maximum (if bounded).
Points of Inflection — The Most Missed Concept

A point of inflection is where the graph changes concavity — from concave up to concave down, or from concave down to concave up. It is NOT a maximum or minimum. It is where the rate of change switches from increasing to decreasing or vice versa.

Point of Inflection — Definition
A point where concavity changes (∪ to ∩, or ∩ to ∪).

At a point of inflection: the function goes from "speeding up" to "slowing down" (or vice versa).

It is NOT a local max or min. The function can be increasing through an inflection point, decreasing through one, or have a flat tangent — but it doesn't change direction of increase/decrease.
The inflection point (gold dot) is where concavity switches. The function is increasing the entire time — the inflection point does NOT mark a direction change, only a concavity change.
❌ Most common error on your exam data: Students identify a point of inflection as a local maximum or minimum. They see the curve "changing shape" and mark it as an extremum. Remember — inflection = concavity change only. Extremum = direction change (increase to decrease or vice versa).

Practice · Extrema & Inflection Points
Problem — AP Style (Practice Exam Q2 Type)
A polynomial function f has exactly two local maxima, one local minimum, and one point of inflection on the interval shown.

The graph shows: f rises to a peak at (1, 6), drops to a valley at (3, 2), rises to a second peak at (5, 8), then descends. The graph has an S-bend between x=2 and x=4 where concavity switches.

Identify: (a) all local maxima with x-values, (b) the local minimum, (c) the approximate location of any inflection points, (d) the global maximum on the visible portion.
1
(a) Local maxima — where does f change from increasing to decreasing?
At (1, 6): f increases before x=1 and decreases after → local max at x = 1, value 6
At (5, 8): f increases before x=5 and decreases after → local max at x = 5, value 8
2
(b) Local minimum — where does f change from decreasing to increasing?
At (3, 2): f decreases before x=3 and increases after → local min at x = 3, value 2
3
(c) Inflection point — where does concavity switch?
The S-bend between x=2 and x=4 shows concavity changing. The inflection point is approximately at x ≈ 3 (in the S-curve region between the peaks and valley).

Note: the inflection point is NOT at the local max or min — it's where the bending direction of the curve switches.
→ Key: inflection points often appear between a local max and local min on a polynomial, but they are at a different x-value than the extrema.
4
(d) Global maximum on visible portion
Compare all local max values: 6 vs 8. The global maximum is at x=5 with value 8 — it's the largest y-value among all local maxima.
Problem
The graph of f shows: starts low, rises steeply, curves over a peak at (2, 10), decreases through an S-bend around x=4, reaches a valley at (6, 1), then rises again. The concavity changes from concave down to concave up somewhere between x=3 and x=5.

(a) Where is the local maximum? (b) Where is the local minimum? (c) Where is the inflection point (approximate)? (d) Is the inflection point a local max, local min, or neither?
(a) Local max: where does the graph peak (change from increasing to decreasing)?
(b) Local min: where does the graph valley (change from decreasing to increasing)?
(c) Inflection point: where does concavity switch? (between x=3 and x=5)
(d) Is the inflection point a local extremum? Why or why not?
(a) Local max at x = 2, f(2) = 10 (function increases before x=2, decreases after)
(b) Local min at x = 6, f(6) = 1 (function decreases before x=6, increases after)
(c) Inflection point at approximately x ≈ 4 (middle of the S-bend where concavity flips from ∩ to ∪)
(d) Neither. At the inflection point, the function is still decreasing — it has not changed direction. An extremum requires a change from increasing to decreasing or vice versa. The inflection point only changes how it's decreasing (from fast-then-slow to slow-then-fast).
Blind — Vocabulary Precision
A student looking at a cubic polynomial graph says: "The curve bends at x=2, so x=2 must be a local maximum."

(a) What error is the student making?
(b) What is x=2 actually called if the curve just bends (changes concavity) there?
(c) Write the conditions that would make x=2 a local maximum instead.
(a) The student is confusing a change in concavity with a change in direction. "The curve bends" means concavity changes — not that the function switched from increasing to decreasing.

(b) If the curve only changes concavity at x=2 (bends but keeps going the same direction), x=2 is a point of inflection.

(c) For x=2 to be a local maximum: the function must be increasing for x < 2 and decreasing for x > 2 — the curve must peak and reverse direction. Just bending without reversing is an inflection point, not a max.
1B.2

Zeros, Multiplicities & Even/Odd Functions

Topic 1.5
Zeros of a Polynomial

A zero (root, x-intercept) of a polynomial f is a value x = c where f(c) = 0. For a polynomial in factored form f(x) = a(x − c₁)ⁿ¹(x − c₂)ⁿ²···, each factor gives a zero.

From Factored Form to Zeros
f(x) = 2(x − 3)²(x + 1)(x − 5)

Zeros: x = 3 (from x−3=0), x = −1 (from x+1=0), x = 5 (from x−5=0)

Degree: 2 + 1 + 1 = 4 (sum of all exponents)
Multiplicity — How the Graph Behaves at Each Zero

The multiplicity of a zero is the exponent on its factor. It controls what the graph does when it reaches that x-intercept.

1
Odd (1, 3, 5...)
Graph crosses the x-axis — goes through it from one side to the other.
2
Even (2, 4, 6...)
Graph touches (bounces off) the x-axis — stays on the same side.
3
Odd ≥ 3 (flattened)
Graph crosses but is flat/tangent at the axis — an S-curve through the zero.
💡 Memory shortcut: Even multiplicity = even = bounce (like an even bounce). Odd multiplicity = odd = odd behavior = it pushes through. Higher odd (3,5) = flattens before crossing, like it's tired.
Even and Odd Functions
Even Function
Symmetric about the y-axis
f(−x) = f(x) for all x
Graph folds onto itself left-right
Examples: x², x⁴, cos x
Odd Function
Symmetric about the origin
f(−x) = −f(x) for all x
Graph rotates 180° onto itself
Examples: x, x³, sin x
⚠️ Even/Odd function ≠ even/odd degree. f(x) = x² + 1 is even (y-axis symmetry). f(x) = x² + x is neither even nor odd — it has even-degree terms mixed with odd-degree terms. To test: substitute −x and check if you get f(x) (even), −f(x) (odd), or neither.

(b) Describe graph behavior at each zero.

x = −2 (from x+2=0), multiplicity 3 (odd)

x = 4 (from x−4=0), multiplicity 1 (odd)

At x = 1 (mult 2, even): graph touches (bounces) — touches the x-axis and turns back
At x = 4 (mult 1, odd): graph crosses cleanly — straight through the axis

(c) Degree

4

f(−x) = (−x+2)³(−x−1)²(−x−4) — this is neither equal to f(x) nor −f(x).
f is neither even nor odd.

Blind — Even/Odd Test
Determine algebraically whether each function is even, odd, or neither:

(a) f(x) = x⁴ − 3x² + 7    (b) g(x) = x³ + 2x    (c) h(x) = x³ + x² − 1
(a) f(−x) = (−x)⁴−3(−x)²+7 = x⁴−3x²+7 = f(x) → EVEN (y-axis symmetry)

(b) g(−x) = (−x)³+2(−x) = −x³−2x = −(x³+2x) = −g(x) → ODD (origin symmetry)

(c) h(−x) = (−x)³+(−x)²−1 = −x³+x²−1. This ≠ h(x) and ≠ −h(x) → NEITHER

Key: for even, all terms must have even exponents. For odd, all terms must have odd exponents. Any mix → neither.
1B.3

End Behavior

Topic 1.6 · Described in Words
The Four End Behavior Cases

End behavior describes what happens to f(x) as x → +∞ and as x → −∞. It is determined by the degree and the sign of the leading coefficient only. Everything else is irrelevant for end behavior.

Even Degree · Positive Leading Coeff
Up on both ends ↑↑
As x→±∞, f(x)→+∞
Example: f(x) = 2x⁴ − x + 1
Even Degree · Negative Leading Coeff
Down on both ends ↓↓
As x→±∞, f(x)→−∞
Example: f(x) = −3x² + 5
Odd Degree · Positive Leading Coeff
Down left, Up right ↓↑
x→−∞: f→−∞   x→+∞: f→+∞
Example: f(x) = x³ − 2x
Odd Degree · Negative Leading Coeff
Up left, Down right ↑↓
x→−∞: f→+∞   x→+∞: f→−∞
Example: f(x) = −x³ + x
The AP Exam Verbal Form — This Is What Gets Missed

The AP doesn't just ask for arrows. It asks you to describe end behavior in words or match verbal descriptions to equations. Here's the exact language structure:

Verbal Template — Fill in the Blanks
"As x increases without bound, f(x) [increases / decreases] without bound."
"As x decreases without bound, f(x) [increases / decreases] without bound."

For f(x) = −2x³ + 5x:
"As x → +∞, f(x) → −∞" → "As x increases without bound, f decreases without bound."
"As x → −∞, f(x) → +∞" → "As x decreases without bound, f increases without bound."
❌ Exact miss from your Q6: "Polynomial Functions - end behavior described." Students see the equation and get the arrows right but then describe them backwards verbally. Practice converting: x → −∞ with f → +∞ means "as x decreases without bound, the function increases without bound."
How to Find the Leading Term

For expanded polynomials, the leading term is the highest-degree term. For factored polynomials, multiply only the leading terms of each factor:

Leading Term from Factored Form
f(x) = −3(x − 2)²(x + 1)³

Leading term = −3 · (x)² · (x)³ = −3x⁵

Degree = 5 (odd), Leading coefficient = −3 (negative)
End behavior: Up left ↑, Down right ↓
