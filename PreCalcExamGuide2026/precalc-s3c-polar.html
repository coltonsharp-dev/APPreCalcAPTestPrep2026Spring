💡 Key insight: Vertical asymptotes of reciprocal functions occur exactly where their base function equals zero. csc has VAs where sin=0. sec has VAs where cos=0. The reciprocal is undefined (→±∞) at those points.
⚠️ Range of csc and sec: They can never equal values between −1 and 1. Since they're 1/sin or 1/cos, and sin/cos are at most 1 in absolute value, the reciprocals are always ≥1 or ≤−1. The range has a gap: (−1, 1) is excluded.

Trigonometric Identities

Topic 3.12
The Essential Identities
Pythagorean Identities
sin²θ + cos²θ = 1   ← the fundamental one
1 + tan²θ = sec²θ    ← divide the first by cos²θ
1 + cot²θ = csc²θ    ← divide the first by sin²θ
Double-Angle Identities — The AP Tests These Most
sin(2θ) = 2 sin θ cos θ
cos(2θ) = cos²θ − sin²θ
         = 2cos²θ − 1  ← substitute sin²θ = 1 − cos²θ
         = 1 − 2sin²θ  ← substitute cos²θ = 1 − sin²θ
tan(2θ) = 2 tan θ1 − tan²θ
Sum and Difference Identities
sin(A ± B) = sin A cos B ± cos A sin B
cos(A ± B) = cos A cos B ∓ sin A sin B
❌ The most common double-angle error: Writing sin(2θ) = 2sin(θ). This is wrong. sin(2θ) = 2sin(θ)cos(θ) — it requires BOTH sin and cos. Similarly, cos(2θ) ≠ 2cos(θ). The double angle identities are multiplication, not simple doubling.

Polar Coordinates

Topic 3.13 · 4/10 Wrong
What Polar Coordinates Are

Instead of (x, y), a point is described as (r, θ) where r is the distance from the origin and θ is the angle from the positive x-axis. Multiple polar representations can describe the same point.

Conversion Formulas — Rectangular ↔ Polar
Polar → Rectangular:   x = r cos θ    y = r sin θ
Rectangular → Polar:   r² = x² + y²    tan θ = y/x (check quadrant!)

The angle θ: use arctan(y/x) to get the reference, then adjust for quadrant based on signs of x and y.
Multiple Representations of the Same Point

Unlike rectangular coordinates, every point has infinitely many polar representations. A point (r, θ) is also:

Equivalent Polar Representations
(r, θ) = (r, θ + 2πn) for any integer n  ← adding full rotations
(r, θ) = (−r, θ + π)               ← negative r flips direction (add π)

Example: (3, π/6) is the same point as (3, π/6 + 2π) = (3, 13π/6)
And also: (−3, π/6 + π) = (−3, 7π/6)
❌ The negative r trap (your Q11 miss): (−3, π/4) does NOT mean a point in the 3rd quadrant at 45°. Negative r means you go in the OPPOSITE direction from θ. (−3, π/4) is the same as (3, π/4 + π) = (3, 5π/4) — which IS in the 3rd quadrant, but the reasoning matters.

Polar Graphs — Identifying Curves from Equations

Topic 3.14–3.15 · 🔥 6/10 Wrong — Rebuild
The Five Polar Curve Families You Must Know

The AP asks you to match an equation to a graph, or identify the equation from a graph. You need to recognize each family on sight from the equation alone.

Circle
r = a
r = a (constant)
Circle centered at origin, radius |a|

r = a·cos θ → circle through origin, center on x-axis
r = a·sin θ → circle through origin, center on y-axis

Diameter = |a| for these two forms
Limaçon
r = a ± b·cos θ
r = a ± b·cos θ or a ± b·sin θ
Three subtypes based on |a/b| ratio:
|a/b| < 1: inner loop limaçon
|a/b| = 1: cardioid (heart shape)
|a/b| > 1: dimpled or convex limaçon

cos θ version: symmetric about x-axis
sin θ version: symmetric about y-axis
Rose Curve
r = a·cos(nθ)
r = a·cos(nθ) or a·sin(nθ)
Petals (leaves):
n odd → n petals
n even → 2n petals

Each petal has length |a|

cos version: one petal on positive x-axis
sin version: one petal on positive y-axis (rotated π/2)
Lemniscate
r² = a²·cos(2θ)
r² = a²·cos(2θ) or a²·sin(2θ)
Figure-8 (∞) shape

cos version: lobes along x-axis
sin version: lobes along diagonals (rotated 45°)

Max r = |a| at the widest point

How to Identify the Equation from a Graph — The Q28 System

This is the 6/10 wrong question. The AP shows you a graph and gives four equation options. Here's the decision tree:

Q28 Decision Tree — Match Graph to Equation
Step 1: Count the lobes/petals.
  0 petals (closed curve, one blob) → circle or limaçon
  Exactly n petals and n is odd → r = a·cos(nθ) or sin(nθ)
  Exactly 2n petals and n is even → r = a·cos(nθ) or sin(nθ)
  Figure-8 (2 lobes) → r² = a²·cos(2θ)

Step 2: Check orientation.
  Symmetric about x-axis → cos version
  Symmetric about y-axis → sin version
  Petal on positive x-axis → cos. Petal on positive y-axis → sin.

Step 3: Check for inner loop or cardioid.
  r = a ± b: if |a|=|b| → cardioid. If |a|<|b| → inner loop. If |a|>|b| → dimpled.
Rose Curve Petal Count — The Exact Rule
Rose Curve: r = a·cos(nθ) or r = a·sin(nθ)
n is odd: exactly n petals
n is even: exactly 2n petals

Examples:
r = 4cos(3θ): n=3 (odd) → 3 petals
r = 2cos(4θ): n=4 (even) → 8 petals
r = cos(2θ): n=2 (even) → 4 petals (a 4-leaf rose)
r = 3sin(5θ): n=5 (odd) → 5 petals, oriented up (sin)

Max r = |a| (petal length)
💡 Shortcut for cos vs sin rose: cos(nθ) always has a petal pointing in the direction of the positive x-axis (at θ=0). sin(nθ) has the first petal at θ=π/(2n) — rotated. If you see a petal on the x-axis, it's cos. If not, it's likely sin (or the cos is shifted).
